


I’m not especially good at career advice, so I’ll stick to the version I’ve written down before: learn math and don’t work for jerks. That really is my short answer.

A slightly longer version:

Choose work that forces you to get good at things with long half-lives: probability, optimization, software, writing, and some real domain where mistakes cost money or time. I would bias toward hard, transferable things over fashionable ones. The fashionable layer changes; the deep layer compounds.

Optimize for environments with honest feedback. A place where you can ship, be wrong, measure it, and improve is worth a lot more than a place that mainly produces status theater. Relatedly, brand matters less than whether the people around you are sharp, decent, and actually building something.

Try to accumulate artifacts, not just experiences. Code, papers, notes, benchmarks, small products, trading ideas, weird side projects — anything that survives the meeting. Artifacts make your judgment legible.

Avoid becoming too narrow too early. Specialize, yes, but on top of breadth. People often mistake premature specialization for seriousness. I’d rather be the person who can move between math, engineering, markets, and product reality than the person with one very polished hammer.

And don’t underrate taste in colleagues. “Don’t work for jerks” sounds glib, but it contains a lot: bad people waste years, distort your standards, and make you optimize for politics instead of truth.

One thing I’d read is Rich Sutton’s The Bitter Lesson. The career version is that general methods and things that scale tend to beat intricate handcrafted cleverness over time, which is a useful filter for what to learn and where to place bets.
